Dinosaurs first appeared during the Triassic Period more than 230 million years ago and diversified into a wide range of body plans, diets, and ecological roles. Early species were generally small and bipedal, but over millions of years some lineages evolved into massive herbivores while others became highly specialized predators. Their evolutionary success was helped by efficient locomotion, varied feeding strategies, and adaptation to changing climates across the supercontinent Pangaea and later separated landmasses.

One of the most recognizable groups is the theropods, which includes famous carnivores like Tyrannosaurus rex as well as the ancestors of modern birds. Theropods show striking diversity, from large apex hunters to feathered forms adapted for gliding and eventually powered flight. Fossil discoveries over the last few decades have strengthened the connection between non-avian dinosaurs and birds by revealing feathers, wishbones, and other bird-like anatomical features in many species.

Herbivorous dinosaurs were equally varied and often lived in large herds. Long-necked sauropods such as Brachiosaurus and Argentinosaurus reached extraordinary sizes, likely using their height and long necks to access vegetation unavailable to other animals. Ornithischian herbivores, including Triceratops, Stegosaurus, and hadrosaurs, developed defensive structures like horns, frills, spikes, and armored plates, which may have served both protective and social display functions.

Dinosaurs inhabited diverse environments, including river plains, coastal regions, forests, and semi-arid landscapes. Paleontologists reconstruct these ancient ecosystems using fossilized bones, footprints, eggs, and even preserved skin impressions. Trace fossils can show behavior that skeletons alone cannot, such as group movement, nesting patterns, and predator-prey interactions, giving researchers a richer view of how dinosaurs lived day to day.

The non-avian dinosaurs went extinct around 66 million years ago at the end of the Cretaceous, likely due to a combination of catastrophic asteroid impact effects and environmental disruption. While many groups disappeared, one branch survived: avian dinosaurs, or birds. This means dinosaurs are not entirely gone, but continue today in an evolutionary sense, making every living bird a reminder of a lineage that has persisted through one of Earth's most dramatic extinction events.
